---
title: Californian Ideology
description: The paradoxical blend of countercultural optimism and market libertarianism that shaped Silicon Valley's worldview, critiqued in a 1995 essay by Barbrook and Cameron.
---

A 1995 essay by British media theorists Richard Barbrook and Andy Cameron that diagnosed the dominant belief system of Silicon Valley: a hybrid of 1960s hippie individualism and 1980s free-market economics, united by faith in technology as the engine of human liberation.

Original essay: [The Californian Ideology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Californian_Ideology) (published in *Mute* magazine, 1995; revised in *Science as Culture*, 1996).

## Core claims

Barbrook and Cameron argued that tech entrepreneurs of the 1990s combined "the free-wheeling spirit of the hippies and the entrepreneurial zeal of the yuppies." The result was an ideology that claimed to be anti-establishment while actively dismantling public institutions and concentrating power in private platforms.

Key features:
- Technological determinism: the net will inevitably flatten hierarchies and liberate individuals
- Anti-statism: government is the obstacle; markets and technology are the solution
- Radical individualism: collective problems dissolve when individuals are empowered by technology
- Paradox: claimed liberation while entrenching corporate control and surveillance

By 2015, *Wired* acknowledged the essay had been "vindicated by the corporate take-over of the Net."

## Connections

The Californian Ideology is a primary source of [[Technosolutionism]] — the belief that technology can solve fundamentally political and social problems. It provides the cultural substrate for that view: if technology is inherently liberating, then political struggle is obsolete.

It is also an expression of [[Neoliberal ideology]] in tech form: markets and entrepreneurs replace the state; collective action problems are reframed as market opportunities or information gaps. The commons is not protected — it is disrupted.
